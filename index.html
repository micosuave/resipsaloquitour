Five (5) Essential Qualities of “The Scientific Lawyer”

Arie M. Michelsohn, Ph.D., J.D.

When I was a graduate student in the Biology Division at Caltech in the 1980s, I had the good fortune to serve as a Teaching Assistant for an upper-level, undergraduate, introductory course in neurobiology, called BI 150.  It was an exciting time for neuroscience, as advancements in our understanding of both neural circuits and computation started to converge into a new field.  

In 1986, Caltech started a new graduate program called Computation & Neural Systems, organized jointly by the Division of Biology, the Division of Engineering and Applied Science, and the Division of Physics, Mathematics and Astronomy.  BI 150 was a required course for first-year graduate students in the program.  This enabled me to witness, and in some small way, participate first-hand, in the dawn of computational neuroscience and the advent of neural-network computation.  

More than a decade later, as a junior lawyer, I had another Forrest-Gump moment:  I got to study a large swath of the then-emerging patent landscape for bioinformatics and computational genomics.  More recently, through a fascinating consulting engagement, I was again able to delve deeply into the history of the convergence of computation; this time with chemistry, in a case involving computer-aided drug design around the turn of the 21st Century. 

As a lawyer, what excites me—most of all—is the convergence of computation and law that we are all experiencing, right now. The digital legal enlightenment may well be the ultimate convergence of the nerds; and that, to my mind, is a great thing.  

Law practice is evolving to encompass a more techno-savvy legal mind-set. I would argue, further, that law practice can productively be viewed as an applied science: The application of science to the pursuit of...Justice. That is, by agile navigation through the multidimensional information-space of modern legal systems on behalf of clients, lawyers add great value to strategic vision, the propriety of business transactions, and risk mitigation in all spheres of complex, modern economic and social life. The nature and substance of the iterative curatorial, compositional, and communication workflows that support the high-value strategic judgments lawyers make can, nowadays, increasingly be modelled, measured, and made much more efficient.

I would like to suggest five qualities of "Scientific Lawyers" that will be keys to the successful, long-term, and productive integration of law and computation in the years to come. These qualities may be considered foundational to an informed discussion about what "machine intelligence" for lawyers is, or should be.

The Scientific Lawyer:

1. Experiments Clearly and Rigorously
2. Engages the Tech Side of the Digital Era
3. Visualizes Legal Data Meaningfully
4. Models Real Problems to Optimize Tractable Solutions
5. Thrives in a Competitive Digital Commons

Today, I would like to elaborate on the first of these qualities. I plan to further discuss each of the other qualities, in turn, over the course of this week.

1. The Experimental Lawyer

In her whimsically informative book, " Much Depends on Dinner," Margaret Visser writes about the history, culture, and science of an ordinary meal.  The discussion about corn, in Chapter 1 (at page 25), provides one of the simplest and most elegant examples of scientific reasoning I have ever seen.  It is a testament to clear and rigorous scientific thinking across any age and discipline:

The Indians observed that different types of corn could cross and produce offspring with characteristics derived from both parents.  They believed that the plant roots mingled underground to produce this effect, and this was not proven wrong until 1724, when Judge Paul Dudley of Massachusetts noticed that changes took place in corn when different types were separated by a river, but not when they were separated by a high fence. The Crossing must therefore take place in the air, and not underground.

The Scientific Lawyer appreciates that legal phenomena, like the "relevance" of a document to discovery in litigation, or a conditional clause in a self-enforcing etherium-blockchain contract, also are amenable to scientific inquiry. Legal phenomena increasingly can be made the subjects of experimental designs that yield reproducible observations and measurements, within measurable uncertainties.  Well-designed experiments can provide dispositive answers to questions of significant importance to the efficient and effective practice of law in the digital era.  

The scientific lawyer further understands that "good" experiments are well-controlled, and strategically designed to distinguish clearly between alternative, causal hypotheses—ideally, validating one, while falsifying another. Science, like law, never really proves something is true to 100% certainty. But it can falsify (or "impeach"), ruthlessly and unambiguously.   See e.g., Richard P. Feynman, " The Essence of Science in 60 Seconds" (Caltech, 1964) (YouTube); see alsoRichard P. Feynman, "On Scientific Method" ( longer segmentfrom same lecture, giving an opportunity to hear an additional five minutes of Professor Feynman discussing the scientific method).

A foundational example of the utility of clear and rigorous legal science, heralding the digital legal era, can be found in the landmark opinion by Judge Andrew Peck of the Southern District of New York, in  Moore v. Publicis Groupe, 11 CIV. 1279 ALC AJP, 2012 WL 607412.  There, Judge Peck quotes from the empirical legal studies published by Grossman & Cormack, Maura R. Grossman & Gordon V. Cormack, Technology-Assisted Review in E-Discovery Can Be More Effective and More Efficient Than Exhaustive Manual Review, Rich. J.L.& Tech., Spring 2011.  

Grossman & Cormack used a carefully-controlled experimental paradigm to measure efficiency and efficacy in identifying relevant documents from a large document collection subject to discovery requests in litigation.  The researchers pitted human lawyers against machines trained by senior lawyers. According to the observed data, the machines won, hands-down. See Moore, Slip. Op.at 19 ("[T]he myth that exhaustive manual review is the most effective—and therefore the most defensible—approach to document review is strongly refuted. Technology-assisted review can (and does) yield more accurate results than exhaustive manual review, with much lower effort.") ( quoting Grossman & Cormack, at 48).  (You will additionally find a compendium of resources from the Moorecase under the Resources tab, above.)

Such well-designed empirical legal studies rival the clarity and rigor of those in respected scientific journals, and justify judicial acceptance and reliance for dispositve adjudication. They set a noble standard for the future development of legal science by both lawyers and jurists in the digital legal era.

Moreover, as Grossman & Cormack note at the end of their paper, the machine-enhanced review "processes found to be superior" did not merely entail "the naive application of technology absent human judgment," but rather required a productively interactive relationship between human lawyer and machine. With such a relationship defined specifically, and deployed successfully, research has been able to turn to more interesting questions, like "which technology-assisted review process(es) will improve most on manual review?" Id.

And on the issue of optimizing efficiency, scientific lawyers can take inspiration from the ingenious ways in which computer scientists have continually striven to improve upon the efficiency of important computational processes, as data size and transformational complexity increase globally over time.  A prime example is sorting algorithms. A clear introduction to the subject can be found in thisYouTube video on sorting library books on a shelf—just like the "old days"—except virtually. Consider also watching this mezmerizingly informative, two-minute YouTube video, which compares 24 different sorting algorithmsas they race to the beat, and to the finish, in parallel with each other, on a single screen.  Sometimes, experimental results are thatclear—as a bell and a whistle, video-game style.  There's no question who wins, and who loses; and now all that's left you want to know is...how?!

More, on that, tomorrow.
